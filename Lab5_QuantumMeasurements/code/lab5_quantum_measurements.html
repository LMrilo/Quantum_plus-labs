Rationale

This laboratory offers an experimental verification and visualization of the fundamental quantum concepts presented in the Fundamental Concepts of Quantum Technologies course. Students will explore superposition, quantum measurement, photon polarization, and electron spin represented on the Bloch sphere. Through guided simulations, students will analyze how measurement affects quantum states, how probabilistic outcomes emerge, and how spin and polarization can be used to encode and manipulate quantum information.

Theoretical Background

The Measurement Postulate

For a qubit in state $|\psi\rangle$ measured in basis $\{|m\rangle\}$, the probability of outcome $m$ and the post-measurement state are: $$P(m) = |\langle m | \psi \rangle|^2 \qquad |\psi\rangle \xrightarrow{\text{measure}} |m\rangle$$ This irreversible projection is state collapse.

Bloch Sphere Parametrization

Any single-qubit pure state can be written as: $$|\psi\rangle = \cos\!\left(\frac{\theta}{2}\right)|0\rangle + e^{i\varphi}\sin\!\left(\frac{\theta}{2}\right)|1\rangle, \qquad \theta \in [0,\pi],\; \varphi \in [0, 2\pi)$$ The six cardinal states are: $|0\rangle$ (North), $|1\rangle$ (South), $|{+}\rangle$ ($+X$), $|{-}\rangle$ ($-X$), $|{i}\rangle$ ($+Y$), $|{-i}\rangle$ ($-Y$).

Pauli Operators & Eigenstates

$$\hat{X} = \begin{pmatrix}0&1\\1&0\end{pmatrix},\quad \hat{Y} = \begin{pmatrix}0&-i\\i&0\end{pmatrix},\quad \hat{Z} = \begin{pmatrix}1&0\\0&-1\end{pmatrix}$$ Measuring in the $Z$-basis collapses to $|0\rangle$ or $|1\rangle$. Measuring in the $X$-basis collapses to $|{+}\rangle$ or $|{-}\rangle$. Since $[\hat{X}, \hat{Z}] = \hat{X}\hat{Z} - \hat{Z}\hat{X} \neq 0$, these observables are incompatible — sequential measurements in different bases destroy prior information.

Activities

Activity 1: State Preparation & Bloch Sphere BLOCH SPHERE
Goal: Build visual intuition for qubit states by placing them on the Bloch sphere and understanding the spherical coordinate parametrization $(\theta, \varphi)$.
Theory: The six standard basis states and their Bloch coordinates:
|0⟩
$\theta=0$
|1⟩
$\theta=\pi$
|+⟩
$\theta=\frac{\pi}{2}, \varphi=0$
|−⟩
$\theta=\frac{\pi}{2}, \varphi=\pi$
|i⟩
$\theta=\frac{\pi}{2}, \varphi=\frac{\pi}{2}$
|−i⟩
$\theta=\frac{\pi}{2}, \varphi=\frac{3\pi}{2}$
Instructions (QuVis Bloch Sphere Simulator):
  1. Open the QuVis Bloch Sphere Simulator (link below).
  2. Prepare each of the six states above by adjusting $\theta$ and $\varphi$ sliders.
  3. Observe how the Bloch vector moves to the poles (for $|0\rangle$, $|1\rangle$) and the equator (for $|{+}\rangle$, $|{-}\rangle$, $|i\rangle$, $|{-i}\rangle$).
  4. Note that global phase is not visible on the Bloch sphere.
Reflection: Why do $|{+}\rangle$ and $|{-}\rangle$ both have $\theta = \pi/2$ but lie on opposite sides of the equator? What does $\varphi$ physically represent?
Activity 2: Measurement in Different Bases BORN RULE
Goal: Measure the same state in the $Z$, $X$, and $Y$ bases and observe how the measurement basis entirely determines the outcome probability distribution.
Theory — Born Rule in Practice:
Prepare $|\psi\rangle = |{+}\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(|0\rangle + |1\rangle)$.
Measuring in the Z-basis: $P(0) = P(1) = 50\%$.
Measuring in the X-basis: $P({+}) = 100\%$, $P({-}) = 0\%$.
Why? Because $\langle{+}|{+}\rangle = 1$ and $\langle{-}|{+}\rangle = 0$.
Instructions:
  1. In the QuVis simulator, prepare the state $|{+}\rangle$ ($\theta=\pi/2$, $\varphi=0$).
  2. Select measurement in the Z-basis. Click "Measure" multiple times. Record the outcomes.
  3. Repeat, now selecting the X-basis. Observe that the result is always $|{+}\rangle$.
  4. Repeat with the Y-basis. Verify that outcomes are again 50/50.
  5. For each trial, record: initial state → basis chosen → outcome probabilities → observed outcome.
Reflection: Why does measuring $|{+}\rangle$ in the X-basis always give the same result? How does this change if you instead prepare $|{-}\rangle$?
Activity 3: Photon Polarization POLARIZATION
Goal: Map classical photon polarization to qubit states. Observe how a polarizer acts as a quantum measurement device and causes state collapse.
Theory — Polarization as Qubit Encoding:
Horizontal polarization $\leftrightarrow |0\rangle$,  Vertical polarization $\leftrightarrow |1\rangle$.
Diagonal ($45°$) polarization $\leftrightarrow |{+}\rangle = \frac{|0\rangle+|1\rangle}{\sqrt{2}}$.
A polarizer oriented at angle $\alpha$ measures in the basis $\{|\alpha\rangle, |\alpha+90°\rangle\}$. A photon passes or is blocked according to $P(\text{pass}) = \cos^2(\alpha - \theta_\text{initial})$ (Malus's Law — the classical limit of the Born rule).
Instructions (Quantum Flytrap Virtual Lab):
  1. Open Quantum Flytrap (link below). Select a single-photon polarization experiment.
  2. Prepare a horizontally polarized photon and pass it through a vertical polarizer. Note: 0% transmission.
  3. Prepare a diagonally polarized photon ($45°$) and measure with a vertical polarizer. Note: 50% transmission.
  4. After passing a $45°$ photon through a vertical polarizer (which gives $|1\rangle$ upon passing), measure again with a horizontal polarizer. Note: 50% again — the state has been reset to $|1\rangle$, not $|{+}\rangle$.
  5. Record each measurement and explain what state the photon is in before and after each polarizer.
Reflection: Why does inserting a polarizer at $45°$ between two crossed polarizers allow light through, even though the crossed polarizers alone block 100% of the light? (Hint: state collapse resets the photon's information.)
Activity 4: Electron Spin & Stern-Gerlach SPIN
Goal: Use the Stern-Gerlach apparatus to observe spin quantization. Understand how spin-up and spin-down states along different axes map to qubit states and Bloch sphere positions.
Theory — Spin and the Bloch Sphere:
The electron spin observable along axis $\hat{n} = (\sin\theta\cos\varphi,\sin\theta\sin\varphi,\cos\theta)$ is: $$\hat{S}_{\hat{n}} = \frac{\hbar}{2}\hat{\sigma}_{\hat{n}} = \frac{\hbar}{2}(\sin\theta\cos\varphi\,\hat{X} + \sin\theta\sin\varphi\,\hat{Y} + \cos\theta\,\hat{Z})$$ The Stern-Gerlach apparatus oriented along $\hat{n}$ splits the beam into spin-up ($+\hbar/2$) and spin-down ($-\hbar/2$) outputs.
Instructions (PhET Stern-Gerlach):
  1. Open the PhET Stern-Gerlach simulation (link below).
  2. Send atoms through a Z-oriented apparatus. Observe the beam splitting into two.
  3. Select only the spin-up output ($|\uparrow_z\rangle = |0\rangle$). Send it through a second Z apparatus. Observe: 100% spin-up. The state is known.
  4. Now send the spin-up beam through an X-oriented apparatus instead. Observe: 50/50 split. Why? $|0\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(|{+}\rangle + |{-}\rangle)$.
  5. Select only $|{+}\rangle$ from the X apparatus, then send through a third Z apparatus. Observe: 50/50 again — the X measurement erased the Z information.
Reflection: How does this sequential Stern-Gerlach experiment demonstrate that $[\hat{S}_x, \hat{S}_z] \neq 0$? What would you expect if $\hat{S}_x$ and $\hat{S}_z$ commuted?
Activity 5: Sequential Measurements & State Collapse NON-COMMUTATIVITY
Goal: Demonstrate that measuring in $Z$, then $X$, then $Z$ again produces random outcomes in the final $Z$ measurement, even if the first $Z$ gave a definite result. This builds concrete intuition for $[\hat{X}, \hat{Z}] \neq 0$.
Theory — Non-Commutativity Proof: $$[\hat{X}, \hat{Z}] = \hat{X}\hat{Z} - \hat{Z}\hat{X} = \begin{pmatrix}0&1\\1&0\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}1&0\\0&-1\end{pmatrix} - \begin{pmatrix}1&0\\0&-1\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}0&1\\1&0\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}0&-2\\2&0\end{pmatrix} \neq 0$$ This means $\hat{X}$ and $\hat{Z}$ cannot be simultaneously diagonalized — they have no common eigenstates.
Instructions (IBM Quantum Composer):
  1. Open IBM Quantum Composer (link below). Start with $|0\rangle$.
  2. First Z-measurement: Add a measurement gate. Run. Note the outcome is always $0$.
  3. X-measurement: After the first circuit, apply $H$ then measure. The outcome is 50/50.
  4. Second Z-measurement: After step 3, apply $H$ again then measure. The outcome is again 50/50 — the X-measurement destroyed the $Z$ information.
  5. Compare to Z→Z→Z: Start fresh. Measure $Z$ twice in sequence. Outcome is always the same (no destruction).
Qiskit Convention: Qiskit uses Little Endian ordering. In a bitstring like |10⟩, the rightmost bit is qubit 0. Keep this in mind when reading circuit measurement results.
Reflection: Express the sequence Z→X→Z algebraically using projection operators. Why does the intermediate X measurement introduce irreducible randomness into the final Z outcome, regardless of the initial state?
